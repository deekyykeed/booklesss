Competitive Strategy

How organisations position themselves within an industry to achieve superior, sustainable performance — and why most strategies fail within three years of implementation.

12 min read Porter · Generic Strategies Zambia context
What is competitive strategy

Positioning for Superior Performance

Competitive strategy answers one question: how does an organisation win? Not win temporarily — but outperform rivals consistently over time. Michael Porter, in Competitive Strategy (1980), argued that superior performance stems from two sources: doing the same things as competitors but more efficiently (cost leadership), or doing different things that buyers value more (differentiation).

The common trap is being stuck in the middle — attempting both simultaneously without committing fully to either. A firm that is neither the cheapest nor genuinely differentiated earns below-average returns because it offers customers no compelling reason to choose it over alternatives.

Key Concept
Porter's three generic strategies: (1) Cost Leadership — lowest cost producer in the industry; (2) Differentiation — unique attributes buyers are willing to pay a premium for; (3) Focus — targeting a narrow segment with either cost or differentiation. Each requires distinct organisational capabilities and trade-offs.
Industry Structure

Porter's Five Forces: Reading the Profit Potential of an Industry

Before choosing a strategy, you need to understand the industry you are competing in. Porter's Five Forces framework reveals why some industries are structurally more profitable than others — and where to position to capture the most value.

Central Force
Competitive Rivalry
Intensity of competition between existing firms. High rivalry compresses margins for everyone.
Example: Mobile money in Zambia — MTN, Airtel, and Zamtel compete aggressively on pricing, making differentiation critical for survival.
Force 1
Threat of New Entrants
How easily new competitors can enter and erode existing players' returns.
E.g. Banking sector: ZRA licensing and capital requirements act as barriers protecting Zanaco and Stanbic.
Force 2
Supplier Power
Suppliers' ability to charge higher prices or reduce quality.
E.g. Zambeef's meat processing depends on cattle farmers — concentrated supply increases supplier leverage.
Force 3
Buyer Power
Buyers' ability to push prices down or demand better quality.
E.g. ZESCO sells power to industrial clients with few alternatives — buyer power is relatively low.
Force 4
Threat of Substitutes
Alternative products that fulfil the same function, limiting how much a firm can charge.
E.g. Solar home systems as a substitute for grid electricity — a growing threat to ZESCO's residential revenue.
Application

First Quantum Minerals: A Case in Cost Leadership

First Quantum Minerals' Kansanshi and Sentinel copper mines operate in a commodity industry where price is set globally — no individual producer controls it. The only durable advantage available is lower cost per tonne of copper produced than rivals. First Quantum achieves this through operational scale, proprietary smelting technology at the Kansanshi smelter, and vertical integration that reduces reliance on external processing.

This is textbook cost leadership: invest in efficiency, maintain scale, and let low cost become the competitive moat. The risk is that technological disruption or a sustained commodity price collapse eliminates the margin buffer that cost leadership provides.

Discussion
1
Zambeef operates across meat processing, retail, and cold storage. Using Porter's generic strategies framework, identify which strategy Zambeef is pursuing. What evidence from its operations supports your choice, and what risks does this create?
2
Assess the threat of new entrants in Zambia's commercial banking sector using Porter's Five Forces. Which barriers to entry are structural (industry-driven) and which are regulatory? What does this mean for Zanaco's long-term competitive position?
Key Terms
TermDefinition
Cost LeadershipA strategy to become the lowest-cost producer in the industry, enabling competitive pricing or superior margins at market price.
DifferentiationCreating products or services perceived as unique industry-wide, for which buyers are willing to pay a premium.
Competitive AdvantageA condition that enables a firm to generate more value than rivals and sustain above-average returns over time.
Five ForcesPorter's framework for analysing industry structure via rivalry, entry threat, substitutes, supplier power, and buyer power.
Stuck in the MiddlePorter's term for firms that neither achieve cost leadership nor meaningful differentiation — resulting in below-average profitability.
