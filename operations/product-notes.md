# Booklesss — Product Philosophy Notes

Raw ideas captured here. These inform how content should be structured and how students should be spoken to.

---

## Founder framing (captured 2026-05-12)

Material should be written as though the student owns a company — not as though they're studying some external corporation. Everyone wants to be a founder; this framing makes the content feel personally relevant.

In onboarding: ask what the student's actual intentions are — their company aspirations, what they want to build. Pull this into every content recommendation, especially when content is pushed to Notebook or any personalised output.

**Booklesss as consultant to the user's goals, not just a notes service.**

---

## FACT sentence at the end of each concept (captured 2026-05-12)

After each key point or concept, add a single sentence stated as a fact — a mini-conclusion that confirms what was just learned and pre-empts the question the student is likely to leave with.

The sentence should be written so the student reads it and thinks "yes" — no ambiguity, no loose ends. This reduces forgetting during that concept window.

Example pattern: concept explained → FACT sentence: "This is why [X] always [Y] when [condition]."

---

## Skeleton course model (captured 2026-05-12)

Don't reveal the course in chunks (traditional: topic by topic, fully, in sequence).

Instead: from day one, give students a full structural overview — they should know what the last section is about before they've started Step 2. Then build on that skeleton, adding depth as they go.

At the end of the course, students don't feel like they're discovering new territory — they're completing a picture they already hold. The learning feels like confirmation and depth-building, not accumulation.

Implication for PDF structure: each step should reference where it sits in the whole course arc. Step 1 should name every other step and where it goes. The "Learning Outcomes" page at the end of each PDF is where this can land.
