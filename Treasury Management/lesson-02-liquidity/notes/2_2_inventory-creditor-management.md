## Inventory Management, EOQ & Creditor Management
BBF4302 Treasury Management

How do organisations decide how much stock to hold, how often to order, and how long to take paying their suppliers?

***

## Why Inventory Matters

Every business holds stock — raw materials waiting to be used, goods being made, finished products ready to sell. Getting the balance right is critical.

Hold **too much**: you tie up cash, pay for storage, and risk stock going obsolete.
Hold **too little**: you run out, miss sales, and disrupt production.

Treasury's job is to find the sweet spot — the level of inventory that keeps operations running without wasting money.

### The 5 Types of Inventory

| Type | What it is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Raw materials | Basic inputs to production | Copper for Konkola Copper Mines |
| Work in progress | Items being manufactured | Half-assembled goods on a factory floor |
| Finished goods | Completed, ready to sell | Stock sitting in a Zambeef warehouse |
| Scrap / obsolete | Damaged or outdated items | Off-spec copper that gets recycled |
| Stores & supplies | Indirect production support | Lubricating oil for ZESCO machinery |

***

## How to Finance Your Inventory

Holding stock costs money — and businesses need a way to fund it. There are five main options:

### Trade Credit (Accounts Payable)
The cheapest and most common option. You buy from suppliers and pay later — effectively free short-term finance. The longer you take to pay, the more working capital you free up.

### Supply Chain Financing
The buyer (usually a large, creditworthy company) arranges financing for the seller. The seller gets paid early at a low interest rate; the buyer keeps cash longer. Common in industries with large anchor buyers.

### Collateralised Loans
The bank lends against the value of your inventory. If you default, the bank takes the stock. Your cash flows are the primary repayment source — the inventory is just security.

### Asset-Based Loans
Similar to collateralised loans but based purely on inventory value — not your overall financial strength. If you default, the lender physically takes possession of the stock.

### Floor Planning
Used for high-value individual items — cars, trucks, heavy machinery. Each unit has its own loan recorded by serial number. The loan is only repaid when that specific item is sold.

> VISUAL [/columns — 3 columns]: Column 1: Trade Credit (cheapest, most flexible). Column 2: Collateralised / Asset-Based (bank takes inventory as security). Column 3: Floor Planning (per-item loans for high-value goods).

## Just In Time (JIT) — Order Only What You Need

JIT is a philosophy: receive materials from suppliers at exactly the moment you need them — not before. The goal is to eliminate idle inventory entirely.

**Core objectives of JIT purchasing:**
- Reduce raw material stock to near zero
- Order frequently in small batches from a small number of trusted suppliers
- Sign long-term contracts so suppliers can plan ahead
- Shift quality inspection responsibility to the supplier

**JIT in production:**
- Minimise idle stock sitting between production stages
- Produce only what is ordered — no speculative output

### Benefits of JIT
- Less cash tied up in inventory
- Lower storage and warehousing costs
- Better product quality — waste is eliminated, problems surface quickly
- More flexible — can respond to small or custom orders

### The Risk
JIT only works if your supply chain is reliable. One delayed shipment stops everything. For Zambian manufacturers, supply chain disruptions, border delays, and infrastructure challenges make pure JIT very difficult.

> VISUAL [/warning]: JIT works brilliantly for Toyota in Japan. It is much harder for a manufacturer in Lusaka depending on imported inputs through Dar es Salaam port. Examiners may ask you to evaluate whether JIT is appropriate for Zambian businesses — always consider local supply chain realities.

## Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) — The Maths of Ordering

EOQ answers one question: **what is the optimal quantity to order each time?**

Order too much → high holding costs (storage, insurance, obsolescence).
Order too little → high ordering costs (placing orders frequently, more admin).

EOQ finds the quantity that minimises the **total** of these two costs.

### The Formula

> EOQ = √(2ac ÷ h)

| Variable | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| a | Annual demand in units |
| c | Cost per order (fixed, regardless of size) |
| h | Holding cost per unit per year |

***

## EOQ — Worked Example

**Zambeef Ltd** needs 1,000 units of packaging material per month.
- Cost per order: **K30** (fixed, regardless of order size)
- Holding cost: **K2.88 per unit per year**

**Step 1 — Calculate annual demand:**

Annual demand (a) = 1,000 × 12 = **12,000 units**

**Step 2 — Apply the formula:**

EOQ = √(2 × 12,000 × 30 ÷ 2.88)

EOQ = √(720,000 ÷ 2.88)

EOQ = √250,000

**EOQ = 500 units**

**Interpretation:** Zambeef should order 500 units each time. That means placing 24 orders per year (12,000 ÷ 500). Average stock held at any time = 500 ÷ 2 = 250 units.

## Creditor Management — The Other Side of the Coin

If debtor management is about collecting money owed to you, creditor management is about managing money you owe to others.

**The temptation:** Treat trade credit as free money and pay suppliers as late as possible — maximising your own cash position.

**The risk:** If your key suppliers run into cash flow problems because you're always paying late, they may reduce your credit terms, cut supply, or go out of business entirely — taking your supply chain with them.

### The Balance Treasury Must Strike

| Paying early | Paying late |
|---|---|
| May earn early payment discounts | Frees up working capital |
| Strengthens supplier relationships | Risks supplier cash flow strain |
| Reduces interest costs on borrowing | May damage credit terms over time |
| Better supply reliability | Could disrupt your supply chain |

> VISUAL [/info]: Creditor management mirrors debtor management — the same principles apply in reverse. What you want from your debtors (prompt payment, good communication), your suppliers want from you.

***

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Inventory | Stock held by a business — raw materials, WIP, finished goods, supplies |
| EOQ | Economic Order Quantity — the order size that minimises total inventory costs |
| Holding cost | The cost of keeping one unit in stock for one year (storage, insurance, obsolescence) |
| Ordering cost | The fixed cost of placing one purchase order, regardless of size |
| JIT | Just In Time — a system where inventory is ordered and received only when needed |
| Trade credit | Credit extended by suppliers — goods delivered now, payment due later |
| Floor planning | Asset-based financing for high-value individual items, tracked by serial number |
| Creditor days | The average number of days a business takes to pay its suppliers |
