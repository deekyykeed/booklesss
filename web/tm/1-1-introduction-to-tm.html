What is Treasury Management?

The treasury department is the financial centre of any organisation. Its core job is to protect the organisation's money and make sure there is always enough cash to keep things running.

Think of it this way: the rest of the business makes decisions — treasury makes sure those decisions can actually be paid for.

Three things treasury is always responsible for:

  • Safeguarding financial assets — cash, investments, receivables
  • Managing liabilities — loans, debt, obligations
  • Ensuring daily liquidity — the organisation never runs out of cash to operate

The structure of a treasury function varies depending on the size, type, and complexity of the organisation.

The 3 Key Decisions of the Finance Function

Treasury sits at the centre of three fundamental financial decisions every organisation must make.

1. Investment Decision — where do we put our resources?

  • Which projects to fund (capital budgeting)
  • How much to invest in working capital (stock, debtors)
  • When to invest or divest

2. Financing Decision — how do we raise the money?

  • Choosing the right mix of debt and equity
  • Understanding cost of funds and building a sound capital structure
  • Managing the difference between profit and cash

3. Dividend Decision — what do we do with profits?

  • How much to pay out vs. retain for growth
  • The level and growth of dividends affects the company's market value

Three Levels of Treasury Work

Treasury staff operate at three levels — from big-picture strategy down to day-to-day tasks.

Strategic

Long-term, board-level decisions:

  • Capital structure of the business
  • Dividend and retention policies
  • Capital raising and assessing likely returns

Tactical

Medium-term, management decisions:

  • Managing cash investments
  • Deciding whether and how to hedge currency or interest rate risk

Operational

Day-to-day execution:

  • Transferring cash between accounts
  • Placing surplus cash in short-term instruments
  • Dealing with banks on transactions

Cost Centre vs Profit Centre

How treasury is measured depends on how it's structured.

Cost Centre — most common

  • Treasury is a support function, not expected to generate profit
  • Funded by an internal budget
  • Risk: management focuses on cutting costs rather than recognising treasury's value

Profit Centre — large corporates and financial firms

  • Treasury expected to generate income through trading, hedging, or FX management
  • Business units charged a market rate for treasury services
  • Risk: temptation to speculate

The 6 Treasury Controls

Because treasury handles large sums of money, strong controls are non-negotiable.

1. Segregation of Duties

Front office (trading) must be separated from back office (settlement). The person making a trade cannot also confirm it.

2. Delegation of Authority

Clear limits on who can authorise what. Decision-making power must sit with the right people.

3. Transaction Limits

Caps on individual transaction sizes. Prevents staff from investing in high-risk instruments.

4. Approvals

All trades approved by a senior manager. A separate person reconciles every transaction.

5. Internal Audits

Regular checks to verify actual transactions match company policy.

6. Automation — STP

Straight-Through Processing automates routine transactions end-to-end, reducing manual errors.

Centralised vs Decentralised Treasury

Should an organisation run treasury from one place, or let each region manage its own?

Centralised — managed from HQ or one country

  • Stronger controls, economies of scale, lower costs, tax advantages for MNCs
  • Less flexibility for local operations

Decentralised — each subsidiary manages its own treasury

  • Local expertise: knowledge of local banking, language, customs
  • Downside: duplication of effort, higher compliance burden

Hybrid / Regional Treasury Centres

  • Large MNCs set up regional centres (e.g. one for Africa, one for Europe)
  • Local input feeds into a central treasury management system
