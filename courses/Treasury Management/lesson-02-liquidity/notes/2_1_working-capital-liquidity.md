## Working Capital & Liquidity Management
BBF4302 Treasury Management

Cash is the lifeblood of any business. This step covers how treasury manages the short-term money that keeps the organisation running day to day.

***

## What is Working Capital?

Working capital is the money available to fund a business's day-to-day operations. Technically it is:

**Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities**

Current assets include cash, stock (inventory), and debtors — things that can quickly be turned into cash. Current liabilities include creditors and short-term loans — obligations that must be paid soon.

If current assets exceed current liabilities, the company has a **surplus** — typically held in bank deposits or investments.

If liabilities exceed assets, the company has a **deficit** — typically funded by a bank overdraft or loan.

## Why Working Capital Matters

Even a highly profitable business can collapse if it runs out of cash. This is called a **liquidity crisis** — and it happens more often than people think.

The treasury department's job is to make sure this never happens.

Poor working capital management means money is **tied up in idle assets** — unsold stock sitting in a warehouse, invoices that haven't been collected, suppliers being paid too quickly. All of this reduces the cash available to keep the business running and invest in growth.

Good working capital management balances two competing goals:

### Liquidity
Having enough cash on hand to meet obligations as they fall due. Safety first.

### Profitability
Not holding more cash than you need. Idle cash earns nothing — it should be working.

## Working Capital Policy

Every company must decide how aggressively or conservatively it manages its working capital. There are three broad approaches:

### Aggressive Policy
Hold minimal safety stock and use short-term debt to finance assets. Lower costs but higher risk — the company cannot respond quickly if demand spikes or a supplier fails. High return, high risk.

### Conservative Policy
Hold large safety stocks and use long-term financing. Safer but more expensive. Lower return, lower risk. A company sitting on excess stock and cash is leaving money on the table.

### Moderate Policy
The balance between the two. Match the financing to the nature of the asset — short-term financing for short-term needs, long-term for long-term.

| | Aggressive | Moderate | Conservative |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stock levels | Low | Medium | High |
| Financing mix | Short-term heavy | Balanced | Long-term heavy |
| Return | High | Medium | Low |
| Risk | High | Medium | Low |

## The Cash Conversion Cycle

The **Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)** measures how long it takes a business to turn its investment in stock and debtors back into cash.

Think of it as the gap between when money goes out (paying for raw materials) and when money comes in (collecting from customers). The longer this gap, the more cash the business needs to keep things running.

**CCC = Days Inventory + Days Receivables − Days Payables**

### Days Inventory
How many days stock sits in the warehouse before being sold.

### Days Receivables
How many days it takes customers to pay after a sale.

### Days Payables
How long the company takes to pay its own suppliers.

You want a **short CCC**. The shorter it is, the less cash you need tied up in the cycle. Stretching supplier payments (Days Payables) and collecting from customers faster (Days Receivables) both shorten it.

## Calculating the CCC — Worked Example

Zambeef Plc has the following figures for the year:

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Inventory | K2,600 |
| Cost of Goods Sold | K9,200 |
| Accounts Receivable | K1,700 |
| Revenue | K15,000 |
| Accounts Payable | K1,600 |

### Step 1 — Days Inventory
Days Inventory = (Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365 = (2,600 ÷ 9,200) × 365 = **103 days**

### Step 2 — Days Receivables
Days Receivables = (Receivables ÷ Revenue) × 365 = (1,700 ÷ 15,000) × 365 = **41 days**

### Step 3 — Days Payables
Days Payables = (Payables ÷ COGS) × 365 = (1,600 ÷ 9,200) × 365 = **63 days**

### Step 4 — CCC
CCC = 103 + 41 − 63 = **81 days**

Zambeef waits 81 days between paying for inputs and receiving cash from sales. Every day this is reduced, less working capital is needed.

## Debtor Management & Cash Discounts

Debtors (customers who owe money) represent a major use of working capital. The longer they take to pay, the more cash is tied up waiting.

To speed up collections, companies offer **cash discounts** — a small reduction in the invoice if the customer pays early.

Credit terms are written as: **2/10 net 30** — meaning: take 2% off if you pay within 10 days, otherwise the full amount is due in 30 days.

### Is the Discount Worth It? — Example

Zanaco Trading is offered terms of 2/10 net 30. Their short-term borrowing rate is 8%. Should they take the discount?

**Cost of NOT taking the discount:**

Cost = D ÷ (100 − D) × 365 ÷ (N − T)

= 2 ÷ 98 × 365 ÷ 20 = **37.2% per year**

Since 37.2% is far higher than Zanaco's 8% borrowing cost, they should **borrow funds and take the discount** — it is much cheaper.

### Managing the Credit Portfolio
- Assess each customer's creditworthiness before granting credit
- Use the Credit Reference Bureau (Zambia) for credit checks
- Set individual credit limits
- Follow up overdue accounts promptly

## Factoring

When a company's invoices are building up and cash is tight, one option is **factoring** — selling your invoices to a specialist finance company (the Factor) in exchange for immediate cash.

### How it works
1. Company issues invoices to customers as normal
2. Factor advances **80–85%** of the invoice value immediately
3. Factor takes over the sales ledger — chasing and collecting payments
4. When customer pays, the Factor remits the remaining balance minus fees

### Fees
- **Administration fee**: 2–3% of invoice value (covers ledger management)
- **Finance charge**: interest on the advance (typically base rate + 3%)
- **Non-recourse option**: for an extra fee, the Factor guarantees payment even if the customer defaults

### Example — Mutengo Plc (ZMW)

Monthly invoices: K300,000 | Credit period: 2.5 months | Factor advance: 85% at 13% p.a.

| | Amount |
|---|---|
| Annual fee (2.5% × K3.6m) | K90,000 |
| Finance charge | K82,874 |
| Total cost | K172,874 |
| Less: admin savings | (K95,000) |
| **Net annual cost of factoring** | **K77,874** |

Compared to borrowing K637,500 from the bank at 12.5% (cost: K79,688) — factoring saves approximately K2,000 per year.

## Invoice Discounting vs Factoring

**Invoice discounting** is a lighter version of factoring. The finance company advances cash against invoices — but the company keeps running its own sales ledger. Customers never know the arrangement exists.

| | Factoring | Invoice Discounting |
|---|---|---|
| Who collects from customers | The Factor | The company itself |
| Customer awareness | Yes — Factor contacts them | No — kept confidential |
| Sales ledger management | Outsourced | Retained in-house |
| Cost | Higher | Lower |
| Best for | Companies wanting full outsourcing | Companies with strong credit control |
| Relationship with customer | Disrupted | Preserved |

Both methods give immediate access to cash tied up in unpaid invoices. The choice depends on whether you want to outsource the collections function or keep it in-house.
