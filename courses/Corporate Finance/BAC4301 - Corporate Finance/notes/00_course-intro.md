### Slide 2 — What This Course Is About
**Title:** What This Course Is About
**Body:** Corporate finance asks three fundamental questions that every business must answer:

1. **Where should the firm invest?** — Capital budgeting and investment appraisal
2. **How should the firm raise money?** — Cost of capital and capital structure
3. **How should profits be returned to shareholders?** — Dividend policy

Beyond these three pillars, this course extends into real-world complexity: valuing companies for takeovers, managing exposure to currency and interest rate swings, and understanding how markets price information.

---

### Slide 3 — Module 1: Investment Appraisal
**Title:** Module 1 — Investment Appraisal
**Body:** The starting point of all corporate finance. This module covers how firms decide which projects to invest in using cash flow analysis and present value techniques.

**Key concepts:**
- Free Cash Flow (FCF) — what it is and how to calculate it
- Net Present Value (NPV) — the gold standard decision rule
- Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR)
- Adjusted Present Value (APV) — handling the tax shield of debt separately
- Capital rationing — choosing the best projects when funds are limited
- NPV in an international context — foreign project appraisal with currency risk

---

### Slide 4 — Module 2: Cost of Capital & Capital Structure
**Title:** Module 2 — Cost of Capital & Capital Structure
**Body:** Once you know how to evaluate a project, you need to know what discount rate to use. This module answers that question — and then asks: does the way a firm is financed actually matter?

**Key concepts:**
- Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)
- Cost of equity (CAPM) and cost of debt
- Credit risk and how it affects the cost of borrowing
- Capital structure — debt vs. equity financing
- Gearing, leverage, and the Modigliani-Miller propositions
- The trade-off between tax shields and financial distress costs

---

### Slide 5 — Module 3: Mergers & Acquisitions
**Title:** Module 3 — Mergers & Acquisitions
**Body:** How do firms grow through acquisition? How is a target company valued? And does the stock market actually reflect all available information?

**Key concepts:**
- Motives for mergers and acquisitions — synergies, diversification, undervaluation
- Valuation methods for target companies — DCF, comparables, asset-based
- Bond valuation and debt securities
- Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) — weak, semi-strong, and strong forms
- Implications of market efficiency for investment strategy and corporate decisions

---

### Slide 6 — Module 4: Interest Rate & Currency Risk Management
**Title:** Module 4 — Interest Rate & Currency Risk Management
**Body:** Real firms operate across borders and borrow at variable rates. This module covers how to identify, measure, and hedge financial risk using derivative instruments.

**Key concepts:**
- Currency risk — transaction, translation, and economic exposure
- Forward contracts and money market hedges for currency risk
- Interest rate risk — how rate changes affect firm value and cash flows
- Hedging instruments: Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs), interest rate swaps, futures, options

---

### Slide 7 — Module 5: Dividend Policy
**Title:** Module 5 — Dividend Policy
**Body:** The final question: once a firm has earned a profit, what does it do with it?

**Key concepts:**
- Dividend relevance vs. irrelevance — Miller-Modigliani dividend theory
- Signalling theory — what dividends communicate to the market
- Dividend policies in practice: stable, constant payout, residual
- Share buybacks as an alternative to dividends
- Tax considerations and shareholder preferences

### Slide 8 — How to Study This Course
**Title:** How to Study This Course
**Body:** Four rules that will make the difference:

1. **Read in order.** Modules are sequenced deliberately — each one builds on the previous.
2. **Do the practice questions.** Every concept requires calculation. Reading without attempting problems will not prepare you for the exam or the assignment.
3. **Note the year on each file.** Some topics have both 2023 and 2024 versions. The newer version is generally preferred, but older versions may contain worked examples not in the newer slides — use both.
4. **Connect the modules.** A real corporate decision rarely sits in one module. An acquisition requires investment appraisal, is affected by financing, may involve foreign targets, and has implications for dividends.
