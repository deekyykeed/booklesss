# BAC4301 — Corporate Finance
## Course Introduction & Navigation Guide

---

## Welcome

This course equips you with the financial decision-making tools used by corporate managers, analysts, and financial professionals. By the end, you will be able to evaluate investment opportunities, determine the right mix of financing, manage financial risk, value companies, and understand how corporate decisions affect firm value.

Start here. Read this document first. It will save you hours of confusion later.

---

## What This Course Is About

Corporate finance asks three fundamental questions:

1. **Where should the firm invest?** — Capital budgeting and investment appraisal
2. **How should the firm raise money?** — Cost of capital and capital structure
3. **How should profits be returned to shareholders?** — Dividend policy

Beyond these three pillars, this course extends into real-world complexity: valuing companies for takeovers, managing exposure to currency and interest rate swings, and understanding how markets price information.

### Module 1 — Investment Appraisal
**Folder:** `01_Investment_Appraisal/`

The starting point of all corporate finance. You will learn how firms decide which projects to invest in using cash flow analysis and present value techniques.

**Key concepts:**
- Free Cash Flow (FCF) — what it is and how to calculate it
- Net Present Value (NPV) and why it is the gold standard decision rule
- Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR)
- Adjusted Present Value (APV) — handling the tax shield of debt separately
- Capital rationing — choosing the best projects when funds are limited
- NPV in an international context — foreign project appraisal with currency risk

### Module 2 — Cost of Capital & Capital Structure
**Folder:** `02_Cost_of_Capital_and_Capital_Structure/`

Once you know how to evaluate a project, you need to know what discount rate to use. This module answers that question and then asks: does the way a firm is financed actually matter?

**Key concepts:**
- Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)
- Cost of equity (CAPM) and cost of debt
- Credit risk and how it affects the cost of borrowing
- Capital structure — debt vs. equity financing
- Gearing, leverage, and the Modigliani-Miller propositions
- The trade-off between tax shields and financial distress costs

**Files to read:**
1. Cost of Capital - Session 1
2. Credit Risk and Cost of Capital - Session 2
3. Capital Structure and Gearing

### Module 3 — Mergers & Acquisitions
**Folder:** `03_Mergers_and_Acquisitions/`

How do firms grow through acquisition? How is a target company valued? And does the stock market actually reflect all available information? This module covers corporate transactions and market theory.

**Key concepts:**
- Motives for mergers and acquisitions (synergies, diversification, undervaluation)
- Valuation methods for target companies (DCF, comparables, asset-based)
- Bond valuation and debt securities
- Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) — weak, semi-strong, and strong forms
- Implications of market efficiency for investment strategy and corporate decisions

**Files to read (Lectures → then Practice Questions):**

### Module 4 — Interest Rate & Currency Risk Management
**Folder:** `04_Interest_Rate_and_Currency_Risk_Management/`

Real firms operate across borders and borrow at variable rates. This module covers how to identify, measure, and hedge financial risk using derivative instruments and market techniques.

**Key concepts:**
- Currency (exchange rate) risk — transaction, translation, and economic exposure
- Forward contracts and money market hedges for currency risk
- Interest rate risk — how rate changes affect firm value and cash flows
- Hedging instruments: Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs), interest rate swaps, futures, options

**Files to read:**
1. Currency Risk and Exchange Rate Risk
2. Forward and Money Market Hedging
3. Interest Rate Risk and Hedging Methods

### Module 5 — Dividend Policy
**Folder:** `05_Dividend_Policy/`

The final question: once a firm has earned a profit, what does it do with it? This module examines the theory and practice of dividend decisions and their effect on shareholder value.

**Key concepts:**
- Dividend relevance vs. irrelevance (Miller-Modigliani dividend theory)
- Signalling theory — what dividends communicate to the market
- Dividend policies in practice: stable, constant payout, residual
- Share buybacks as an alternative to dividends
- Tax considerations and shareholder preferences

## How to Study This Course

**Read in order.** Modules are sequenced deliberately — APV in Module 1 relies on WACC from Module 2 only conceptually; in practice, WACC grounds APV, so read Module 1 and Module 2 together if you find yourself confused about discount rates.

**Do the practice questions.** Every concept in this course requires calculation. Reading without attempting problems will not prepare you for the exam or the assignment.

**Note the year on each file.** Some topics have both 2023 and 2024 versions of slides. The newer version is generally preferred, but the 2023 versions may contain worked examples not in the newer slides — use both.

**Connect the modules.** A real corporate decision rarely sits in one module only. An acquisition (Module 3) requires investment appraisal (Module 1), is affected by how the deal is financed (Module 2), may involve foreign targets (Module 4), and has implications for dividends (Module 5).

## Quick Reference — Key Formulas

| Concept | Formula |
|---|---|
| NPV | NPV = Σ [FCFt / (1+r)^t] − Initial Investment |
| APV | APV = Base-case NPV + PV of Financing Side Effects |
| WACC | WACC = (E/V) × Re + (D/V) × Rd × (1−T) |
| CAPM (Cost of Equity) | Re = Rf + β × (Rm − Rf) |
| MIRR | MIRR = [FV of cash inflows / PV of cash outflows]^(1/n) − 1 |
| Bond Price | P = Σ [Coupon / (1+r)^t] + [Face Value / (1+r)^n] |
