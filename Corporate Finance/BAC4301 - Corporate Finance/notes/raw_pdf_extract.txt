--- p12 ---
Problems with IRR method of appraisal
1. The decision rule is not always clear cut.
▪
For example, if a project has 2 IRRs (or more), it is difficult
▪
to interpret the rule which says "accept the project if the
IRR is higher than the cost of capital".
12
--- p13 ---
Problems with IRR method of appraisal
2. IRR is often mistakenly assumed to be a measure of the
▪
return from a project, which it is not.
The IRR only represents the return from the project if funds
▪
can be reinvested at the IRR for the duration of the project.
13
--- p14 ---
Problems with IRR method of appraisal
Since projects can have multiple IRRs (or none) it is difficult
▪
to meaningfully compare projects using IRR.
Therefore, NPV of the project is considered more reliable for
▪
investment appraisal purposes.

Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR)
The modified internal rate of return (MIRR) is the return
▪
on an investment, considering both the cash flows of the
investment and the earnings on these cash flows based on a
specific reinvestment rate.

--- p16 ---
.
Steps in calculating Modified IRR
Step 1: Calculate the present value of the outflows, discounted at
▪
the project’s cost of capital. PV = FV/(1+r)^n
Step 2: Calculate the future value of the inflows, compounded at
▪
the reinvestment rate . FV = P*(1 + r)^n
Step 3: Calculate the return on the investment.
▪
Step 4: A project is acceptable if MIRR is greater than the
▪
projects cost of capital

Adjusted Present Value (APV)
The APV method is an approach that evaluates the project and
▪
the impact of the financing separately.
Used if a new project has a different financial risk (debt to
▪
equity ratio) from the company, i.e., the overall capital structure
of the company changes.
4
--- p5 ---
Adjusted Present Value (APV)
APV consists of two different decisions:
▪
APV = Investment decision + Financing decision
▪
= Base case npv + PV of financing side effects.
▪
Value of a geared project = Value of an all-equity financed
▪
project + Present value of financing side effects.
Value of an all equity financed project = the base case NPV.
▪
5
--- p6 ---
APV-Investment decision
The project is evaluated as though it were being undertaken by
▪
an all equity company with all financing side effects ignored.
Therefore:
▪
Once the base case NPV is identified, the PV of the financing side
o
effects is evaluated.
Use a beta that reflects just the business risk, i.e. asset beta.
o
Asset beta is used to get the cost of equity used in the base case
o
NPV calculation.
6
--- p7 ---
APV-Financing decision
Financing cash flows consist of:
▪
Issue costs (of equity and or debt)
o
Tax reliefs (savings).
o
As all financing cash flows are low risk (mostly predictable) they
▪
are discounted at either:
The cost of debt or
o
The risk-free rate.
o
PV of financing cash flows is then added to the base case NPV to
▪
find the APV.

======== Capital Rationing (2023).pdf ========
--- p1 ---
Capital rationing
Capital rationing problems exist when there are insufficient
▪
funds available to finance all available profitable projects.
Capital rationing may occur due to internal factors (soft capital
▪
rationing) or external factors (hard capital rationing).
When capital rationing is present across multiple periods, linear
▪
programming may be used to solve the problem of which
projects to undertake.
1
--- p2 ---
Capital rationing
Companies do not have inexhaustible resources therefore it is
▪
likely that only limited funds will be available for capital
investment in each period.
The problem facing financial managers is how best to spend the
▪
money in order to meet their objective of maximising
shareholders' wealth.
This problem can be solved using linear programming.
▪

(Y1 * 10,000) + (Y2 * 0) + (Y3 * 6,000) + (Y4 * 6,000) ≤ 14,000

======== NPV for International Projects (2023).pdf ========
--- p1 ---
Appraising foreign projects
Foreign projects can be evaluated using ordinary appraisal methods.
▪
The NPV of an international project can be calculated either by
▪
converting the cash flows or the NPV.
The main risks faced by companies dealing with foreign currencies
▪
are transaction, translation, and economic risks.
Multinational companies will have access to international debt
▪
facilities, such as Eurobond and syndicated loans..
1
--- p2 ---
NPV for international projects (1)
First approach
▪
(a) Forecast foreign currency cash flows including inflation.
▪
(b) Forecast exchange rates over the projects life
▪
(c) Determine the home currency cash flows using (a) and (b)
▪
above.
(d) Discount the home currency cash flows using the domestic
▪
cost of capital
2
--- p3 ---
NPV for international projects (2)
Second approach
▪
(a) Forecast foreign currency cash flows including inflation
▪
(b) Discount at foreign currency cost of capital and calculate
▪
the foreign currency NPV
(c) Convert into the foreign currency NPV to a home
▪
currency NPV at the spot exchange rate
3
--- p4 ---

▪

▪
bonds of the same maturity.

Yield on corporate bond = risk free rate + credit spread

Relevance theory or traditional view.
The point at which WACC is lowest is the level at which company
▪

▪

Since the value of the firm is the sum of the equity value and the
▪

This can be achieved by minimizing the overall cost of capital
▪
which will give higher present values for all future cash flows.

In this sense, business valuation is considered by many to be

--- p2 ---
Target company valuation
There are two broad approaches to valuing a company.
▪
Asset-based valuations focus on the value of the company’s assets.
▪
Income-based valuations (going concern valuations):
▪
Consider the future earnings or cash flows expected to be obtained
o
by gaining control of the target company.
Each valuation method has advantages and disadvantages and
▪
will be more or less appropriate according to the intentions of the
acquirer towards its target.

--- p3 ---
Stock market value (1)
Stock market value or market capitalisation is the number of issued
▪
ordinary shares of the target company multiplied by their market price.
Market capitalisation = Market price * number of issued shares
▪
Whether stock market value is a fair value will depend on the efficiency of
▪
the stock market.
Stock market value provides a guide to the bidding company of the
▪
minimum likely purchase price of the target company.
It does not give an estimate of how much the target company is worth to
▪
the bidder since it does not reflect bidder’s post-acquisition intentions.
3
--- p4 ---
Stock market value (2)
Stock market value is therefore a useful starting point for estimating the
▪
price that will be paid for the target company as it represents the
minimum that target shareholders will accept.
A substantial premium will need to be added as an incentive to persuade
▪
the shareholders to relinquish their shares.

--- p5 ---
Stock market value (3)
A company’s quoted share price does not reflect the value of all of its
▪
shares since only a small proportion of them are traded at any one time.
The quoted share price reflects only marginal trading.
▪
Therefore there is need to investigate the movement of the target
▪
company’s share price over a period of time.

======== Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) (2023).pdf ========
--- p1 ---
Efficient market hypothesis (EMH)
▪ EMH is based on the assertion that security prices are always in an
equilibrium.
▪ The required rate of return is equal to the expected return. (stocks
intrinsic value is equal to the market price).
▪ The NPVs of all tradable securities in an efficient market is zero.
▪ It is impossible for an investor to consistently ‘beat the market’ and
earn a supernormal profit compared to what is expected given the risk.
▪ Market efficiency is identified as weak form, semi-strong form, and
strong form efficiency.
1
--- p2 ---
Weak form efficiency
▪ Is based on the premise that the current security prices already
incorporates past data (volume of activity and past prices).
▪ The implication is that no investment analyst can consistently ‘beat the
market’ by using historic data.
▪ Price movement is considered to be random (taking a random walk).
▪ No recurrent and predictable patterns in stock prices.
▪ Thus technical analysts(chartists) cannot consistently outperform the
market when picking securities
▪ Technical analysis has no merit in a weak form efficient market.
2
--- p3 ---
Semi-strong form efficiency
▪ Is based on the belief that current security prices reflects all publicly
available information.
▪ In this case no investment analyst (fundamental analysts) equipped with
publicly available information can outperform the market in picking
stocks.
▪ Security prices adjust quickly in response to available information.
▪ Stock markets are considered to be semi-strong form efficient.
3
--- p4 ---
Semi-strong form efficiency cont.
▪ No incorrectly priced securities based on publicly available information.
▪ Share price reaction will occur to and around public announcements.
▪ Thus, there is no best time for managers to issue securities in order to get
the best price.
▪ Reading newspapers is unlikely to add value to any investment strategy.
4
--- p5 ---
Strong form efficiency
▪ Is based on the premise that the current market prices of securities reflect
all important information whether publicly held or private.
▪ In such a market, insiders equally find it impossible to earn consistently
abnormal returns in the stock market.
▪ Prices respond quickly to available new information whether private or
public.
▪ Inside trading is the use of classified data for personal gain.
▪ Inside trading is a criminal offence the world wide.
5

Modigliani and Miller argued that in a perfect capital market (no

Residual theory
It argues that dividends are important but the pattern of them is not.
▪
The residual theory argues that provided the present value of the
▪
dividend stream remains the same, the timing of the dividend payments is
irrelevant.
It follows that only after a firm has invested in all positive NPV projects
▪
should a dividend be paid if there are any funds remaining.
Retentions should be used for project finance with dividends as a
▪
residual.
This theory still takes some assumptions like no taxation and no market
▪
imperfections.

Practical influences on dividend policy
Legal position:
▪
Dividends can only be paid out of profits irrespective of the firm's
▪
liquidity position. No profits no dividends.
Profitability
▪
All other things being equal, the more stable the profit the greater the
▪
proportion that can be safely paid out as dividends.
If profits are volatile it is unwise to commit the firm to a higher
▪
dividend payout ratio.

--- p13 ---
Practical influences on dividend policy
Inflation
▪
In periods of inflation, paying out dividends based on historic cost
▪
profits can lead to erosion of the operating capacity of the business.
For example, insufficient funds may be retained for future asset
▪
replacement.
Growth
▪
Rapidly growing companies commonly pay very low dividends, the
▪
bulk of earnings being retained to finance expansion.

Practical influences on dividend policy
Dividend Signaling
▪
In reality, investors do not have perfect information concerning the
▪
future prospects of the company. Thus, the pattern of dividend is
perceived to provide information of the firm's future performance.
Preference for current income
▪
Many investors (individuals and firms) require cash dividends to
▪
finance current consumption.

Practical influences on dividend policy
Control: The use of internally generated funds does not alter ownership or control.
▪
This can be advantageous particularly in family owned firms.
▪
Liquidity: Sufficient liquid funds need to be available to pay the dividend and fund
▪
working capital requirements.
Tax
▪
The personal tax position of investors may put them in a position of preferring
▪
either dividend income or capital gains through growing share prices.
If the clientele of investors in the company have a clear preference for one or the
▪
other, the company should be wary of altering dividend policy and upsetting
investors.

Practical influences on dividend policy
Other sources of finance
▪
If a firm has limited access to other sources of funds, retained
▪
earnings become a very important source of finance.
Dividends will therefore tend to be small. This situation is commonly
▪
experienced by unquoted companies that have very limited access to
external finance

--- p17 ---
Dividend capacity
This the firm's ability to pay dividends to its shareholders.
▪
It has a direct impact on a company's ability to implement its
▪
dividend policy.
Legally, the firm's dividend capacity is determined by the amount of
▪
accumulated distributable profits.
Practically, the dividend capacity can be calculated as the Free Cash
▪
Flow to Equity (after reinvestment), in that the cash available
determines what the firm can afford to pay.
