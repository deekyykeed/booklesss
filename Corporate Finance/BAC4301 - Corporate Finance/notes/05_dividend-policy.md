**Title:** Module 5 — Dividend Policy

**Body:** Once a firm has earned a profit, what does it do with it? This module examines the theory and practice of how firms decide how much to return to shareholders — and in what form.

### Slide 2 — The Dividend Decision
**Title:** What is Dividend Policy?
**Body:** Dividend policy is the set of rules a firm uses to decide:
- **How much** of its earnings to distribute to shareholders
- **In what form** — cash dividends, share buybacks, or other alternatives
- **When** — frequency and timing of payments

The central question of this module: **does dividend policy actually matter?** Does paying dividends increase, decrease, or leave unchanged the market value of the firm?

Two schools of thought give opposite answers — and both are well-supported.

**Key definitions:**
- **Final dividend** — declared at the end of the financial year, approved by shareholders at the AGM
- **Interim dividend** — paid during the year before full-year profits are known
- **Special dividend** — a one-off, non-recurring payment, usually following an asset sale or exceptional profit
- **Dividend per share (DPS)** — total dividends paid / number of ordinary shares

---

### Slide 3 — Dividend Irrelevance Theory (Modigliani-Miller)
**Title:** MM Dividend Irrelevance Theory
**Body:** Modigliani and Miller (1961) argued that in a **perfect capital market**, dividend policy is irrelevant to firm value. Shareholders are indifferent between dividends and capital gains — they can manufacture their own "homemade dividend" by selling shares.

**Assumptions of the MM model:**
- No taxes (or dividends and capital gains are taxed equally)
- No transaction costs (shares can be bought or sold costlessly)
- Perfect information (all investors have the same information)
- Investment policy is fixed (dividends don't affect the firm's real investments)

**The logic:**
If a firm pays out more in dividends, it must raise more new equity (since investment is fixed). The new share issue dilutes existing shareholders by exactly the amount of the dividend paid. Value is simply transferred — not created.

**Implication:** Managers should focus entirely on investment decisions, not dividend decisions.

---

### Slide 4 — Dividend Relevance: Why Policy Matters in Practice
**Title:** Why Dividend Policy May Matter
**Body:** MM's perfect-market assumptions do not hold in reality. Several real-world frictions make dividend policy relevant:

**1. Taxes**
If dividends and capital gains are taxed differently, shareholders will prefer the lower-taxed form of return. In the UK, dividend income and capital gains are taxed at different rates — shareholder preference matters.

**2. Signalling (information asymmetry)**
Managers have more information about the firm's prospects than investors. A dividend increase signals management confidence in future earnings. A cut signals financial difficulties. Dividends convey credible information that the market would not otherwise have.

**3. Clientele effect**
Different investors prefer different payout policies — pension funds prefer regular income (high dividends); growth investors prefer reinvestment (low dividends). A firm attracts a "clientele" that matches its policy. Changing policy disrupts the clientele.

**4. Agency costs**
Paying dividends forces managers to seek external finance if they want to invest — subjecting their decisions to market scrutiny. This reduces free cash flow available for wasteful spending (Jensen's free cash flow hypothesis).

### Slide 5 — Residual Theory of Dividends
**Title:** Residual Theory
**Body:** The residual theory argues that dividends should be paid only from **earnings left over after all positive-NPV investment opportunities have been funded.**

**Logic:**
Retained earnings are the cheapest source of finance (no issuance costs, no signalling). If the firm has good projects, it should reinvest. Only residual earnings — those not needed for investment — should be paid as dividends.

**Implication:** Dividends are a by-product of the investment decision, not a policy decision in their own right.

**Problem in practice:**
- Dividends become erratic and unpredictable
- Markets interpret volatile dividends as a negative signal
- Clientele investors who rely on stable income are harmed

The residual theory is more useful as a **conceptual baseline** than as a practical policy guide.

### Slide 6 — Dividend Capacity
**Title:** Dividend Capacity
**Body:** Before declaring a dividend, firms must determine how much they **can safely afford to pay** — this is dividend capacity.

**Dividend capacity is determined by:**

1. **Distributable reserves** — under company law, dividends can only be paid from accumulated realised profits (retained earnings), not from capital. A legal maximum exists.

2. **Cash availability** — profits do not equal cash. A profitable firm may have insufficient liquid cash to pay a dividend (e.g. if profits are tied up in inventory or receivables).

3. **Loan covenants** — debt agreements often restrict dividend payments to protect lenders. Maximum dividend clauses are common.

4. **Investment requirements** — if major capital expenditure is planned, the firm may retain earnings rather than distribute them.

5. **Future commitments** — leases, obligations, and pension contributions reduce distributable cash.

**Key ratio:**
**Dividend cover = EPS / DPS** (or Net profit / Total dividend)
A cover of 2× means profits are twice the dividend — considered adequate. Below 1.5× may indicate the dividend is at risk.

### Slide 7 — Dividend Policies in Practice
**Title:** Dividend Policies in Practice
**Body:** Firms adopt explicit or implicit dividend policies to manage investor expectations:

**1. Stable (constant) dividend policy**
Pay a fixed or slowly growing dividend per share each year regardless of year-to-year profit volatility. Most common among large, mature companies. Provides predictability and maintains the investor clientele.

**2. Constant payout ratio**
Pay a fixed percentage of earnings each year (e.g. always pay 40% of EPS). Dividends fluctuate with earnings — transparent but can be volatile and confuse income-seeking investors.

**3. Residual policy**
As described above — pay only what is left after funding all positive-NPV projects. Dividend varies based on investment opportunities.

**4. Zero dividend policy**
Pay no dividends at all — reinvest all profits for growth. Common in early-stage or high-growth firms (e.g. technology companies). Shareholders expect capital gains instead.

**5. Progressive (steadily increasing) policy**
Commit to increasing dividends every year, even if by a small amount. Very popular with UK blue-chip companies. Sends a strong positive signal but creates pressure — cuts are very costly in market terms.

### Slide 8 — Share Buybacks
**Title:** Share Buybacks as an Alternative to Dividends
**Body:** A **share buyback** (share repurchase) occurs when a company uses its cash to buy back its own shares from the open market or from shareholders directly.

**Why firms use buybacks instead of (or in addition to) dividends:**

- **Flexibility** — buybacks are a one-off action and do not create an expectation of ongoing payments
- **Tax efficiency** — in many jurisdictions, capital gains are taxed more favourably than dividend income
- **EPS enhancement** — reducing the share count mechanically increases earnings per share (if profits are unchanged)
- **Signalling** — management signals that shares are undervalued
- **Returning surplus cash** — efficient use of excess cash when investment opportunities are limited

**Methods of buyback:**
- Open market purchases (most common — bought gradually through a broker)
- Tender offer — the company invites shareholders to sell at a fixed premium price
- Direct negotiation — agreed repurchase from a specific large shareholder

**Drawback:** Buybacks may signal a lack of investment opportunities. If funded by debt, they increase financial gearing and risk.

### Slide 9 — Alternative Distribution Methods
**Title:** Other Alternatives to Cash Dividends
**Body:** Beyond cash dividends and buybacks, firms have other ways to distribute value or preserve cash:

**Scrip dividend (stock dividend)**
Shareholders receive new shares instead of cash. The firm conserves cash; shareholders receive equity but no immediate income. Common when the firm needs to retain liquidity.

**Scrip issue (bonus issue / capitalisation issue)**
New shares are issued free to existing shareholders in proportion to their holdings (e.g. 1-for-4). No cash changes hands. This is not a return of value — it simply divides the pie into more, smaller slices. Share price falls proportionally.

**Stock split**
Similar to a scrip issue — the nominal value of each share is reduced and the number of shares increases (e.g. 2-for-1 split). Improves liquidity and marketability of shares by reducing the per-share price.

**Special (one-off) dividend**
A non-recurring cash payment from exceptional profits or asset disposals. The market typically understands it is one-off and does not capitalise it as a recurring stream.

### Slide 10 — Practical Influences on Dividend Policy
**Title:** What Drives Dividend Decisions in Practice?
**Body:** Lintner's classic study (1956) identified that managers:
1. Have a **target long-run payout ratio**
2. Change dividends **gradually** towards the target rather than making sudden jumps
3. Are **extremely reluctant to cut** dividends — even if earnings fall temporarily

This behaviour reflects the signalling power of dividends. In practice, dividend decisions are influenced by:

- **Shareholder expectations** — income investors depend on dividends; cutting them triggers share price falls disproportionate to the fundamental impact
- **Competitors' policies** — sector norms create benchmarks
- **Earnings stability** — volatile earnings make it harder to commit to a progressive policy
- **Gearing levels** — highly geared firms have less flexibility; covenants may restrict payouts
- **Life cycle stage** — mature firms pay more; growth firms pay less or nothing
- **Legal constraints** — dividends cannot exceed distributable reserves
- **Macroeconomic conditions** — recessions force cuts; booms enable increases
