### Slide 2 — Mergers vs. Acquisitions
**Title:** Mergers & Acquisitions — The Basics
**Body:** **Merger:** A friendly reorganisation where two firms combine into a single new entity (A + B = C). Typically involves similarly sized firms. Both sets of shareholders agree.

**Acquisition (Takeover):** One company acquires control of another by purchasing its ordinary shares. The target company ceases to exist as an independent entity. The acquirer is usually larger.

**Forms of takeover:**
- **Horizontal** — two firms in the same industry at the same production stage
- **Vertical** — two firms at different stages of the same production chain (forward = securing distribution; backward = securing raw materials)
- **Conglomerate** — two firms in unrelated industries

### Slide 3 — Motives for M&A
**Title:** Why Do Firms Merge or Acquire?
**Body:** **Economic motives:**
- **Synergy** — combined output exceeds the sum of two separate firms (2 + 2 = 5 effect). Revenue synergy or cost synergy.
- **Economies of scale** — larger combined operations reduce unit costs
- **Elimination of poor management** — replacing underperforming managers is easier via takeover than a shareholder vote
- **Market entry** — quicker and cheaper than organic growth
- **Market power** — increased market share and competitive strength

**Financial motives:**
- **Financial synergy** — lower combined cost of capital
- **Target undervaluation** — the market has mispriced the target (implies market inefficiency)
- **Tax benefits** — acquiring a firm with tax losses to offset future profits
- **Bootstrapping** — increasing earnings per share through acquisition (EPS effect)

**Managerial motives (agency problems):**
- Managers seeking greater power, pay, or job security by growing the firm

### Slide 4 — Financing an Acquisition
**Title:** How Acquisitions Are Financed
**Body:** There are four main payment methods when acquiring a target company:

1. **Cash offer** — Target shareholders receive cash. Simple, certain, but uses the acquirer's liquidity or requires debt financing.
2. **Share-for-share exchange** — Target shareholders receive new shares in the acquiring company. No cash outflow, but dilutes existing shareholders.
3. **Security package** — Payment using debt instruments (bonds, loan notes) or preference shares rather than ordinary shares.
4. **Mixed bid** — A combination of cash and shares, giving target shareholders flexibility.

The method of payment signals the acquirer's confidence — cash offers are seen as strong signals; share offers may imply the acquirer's shares are overvalued.

### Slide 5 — Valuing the Target: Asset-Based Methods
**Title:** Target Valuation — Asset-Based Approaches
**Body:** Asset-based valuations focus on the value of what the target owns:

**1. Net asset value (book value)**
Non-current assets + Current assets − Total liabilities
Uses historical cost figures — simple but often understates true value. Ignores intangibles like brands and goodwill.

**2. Net realisable value (liquidation value)**
The amount receivable from selling all assets on the open market, net of liquidation costs and liabilities. Appropriate if the acquirer intends to break up the business.

**3. Replacement cost**
The cost of acquiring the same assets separately on the open market. More relevant than book value but difficult to determine without inside information.

All asset-based methods provide a **lower bound** for the target's value. A going-concern acquisition should be worth more than break-up value.

### Slide 6 — Valuing the Target: Income-Based Methods
**Title:** Target Valuation — Income-Based Approaches
**Body:** Going-concern valuations reflect the target's future earnings potential:

**1. Price/Earnings (P/E) ratio method**
Share price = EPS × P/E ratio

Use the bidder's P/E, the target's P/E, or the sector average P/E ratio.

**2. Dividend Growth Model (DGM)**
Po = D₀ × (1 + g) / (Re − g)

Where Re is determined using CAPM and g is estimated from historical dividend growth.

**3. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) valuation**
Project the target's future free cash flows, discount at an appropriate rate (WACC or CAPM-derived Re), and sum to get total enterprise value.

**Market capitalisation = Issued shares × Market price per share**

This is the minimum the target's shareholders will accept — a premium must be offered above this to incentivise acceptance.

### Slide 7 — Takeover Defences
**Title:** Defending Against a Hostile Takeover
**Body:** When a board considers a bid hostile, it can deploy defensive tactics:

| Defence | Description |
|---|---|
| **Golden parachute** | Large compensation payments to senior managers if they lose their positions post-takeover — makes the deal more expensive |
| **Poison pill** | Gives existing shareholders the right to buy new shares at a deep discount if a hostile bid succeeds — dilutes the acquirer's stake |
| **White knight** | Invite a friendly counter-bidder to make a better offer and preserve the business |
| **Crown jewels** | Sell or lock up the most attractive assets — makes the firm less appealing as a target |
| **Litigation / Regulatory challenge** | Challenge the bid through competition regulators or the courts |

### Slide 8 — Bond Valuation
**Title:** Valuation of Debt Securities (Bonds)
**Body:** A bond is a negotiable debt instrument issued by governments or corporations. Its value is the **present value of all future cash flows**:

**Zero-coupon bond:** Po = Face value / (1 + r)ⁿ

**Perpetual (irredeemable) bond:** Po = Coupon payment / r

**Redeemable bond:** Po = Σ [Coupon / (1+r)ᵗ] + [Redemption value / (1+r)ⁿ]

Key features:
- **Par value** — face value repaid at maturity
- **Coupon rate** — fixed interest rate (does not change with market rates)
- **Redemption value** — repayment amount (usually par; sometimes at a premium)
- **YTM** — the IRR of the bond's cash flows; equates bond price to PV of all future cash flows

**Inverse relationship:** When market interest rates rise, bond prices fall — and vice versa. At issuance, if the coupon rate = market rate, the bond trades at par.

### Slide 9 — Bond Duration
**Title:** Bond Duration
**Body:** Duration measures the **sensitivity of a bond's price to changes in interest rates** — it is the weighted average time until a bond's cash flows are received.

**Duration = Σ (PV of cash flow × t) / Bond price**

Where t = the time period of each cash flow.

Key points:
- Duration is expressed in **years**
- A bond with duration of 3.5 means its price will change by approximately 3.5% for every 1% change in interest rates
- **Longer maturity → higher duration → more price volatility**
- **Higher coupon rate → lower duration** (more cash flows received early)
- A zero-coupon bond's duration equals its maturity (all value realised at the end)

Duration is the key tool for **interest rate risk management** — matching the duration of assets and liabilities immunises a portfolio against rate changes.

### Slide 10 — Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
**Title:** Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
**Body:** The EMH states that **security prices always fully reflect all available information**. In an efficient market:
- The required return equals the expected return
- NPV of all traded securities is zero
- No investor can consistently beat the market

**Three forms of efficiency:**

**Weak form** — Prices reflect all **past price and volume data**. Technical analysis (chartism) cannot consistently outperform the market.

**Semi-strong form** — Prices reflect all **publicly available information** (past prices + all public announcements, reports, news). Fundamental analysis cannot consistently outperform. Price adjusts immediately to new public information.

**Strong form** — Prices reflect **all information**, both public and private (insider). Even insiders cannot consistently earn abnormal returns. (Note: insider trading remains illegal worldwide.)
