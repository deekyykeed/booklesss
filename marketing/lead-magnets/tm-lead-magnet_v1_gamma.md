## 1. The Cash Conversion Cycle

The CCC measures how long money is locked up before a business gets paid back. It pulls three things together into one number that tells you how efficiently a company runs.

### The Formula

**CCC = Days Inventory + Days Receivables − Days Payables**

The lower the number, the less cash the business needs to borrow to keep running.

### Zambeef Worked Example

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Inventory | K2,600 |
| Cost of Goods Sold | K9,200 |
| Receivables | K1,700 |
| Revenue | K15,000 |
| Payables | K1,600 |

**Days Inventory** = K2,600 ÷ K9,200 × 365 = **103 days**

**Days Receivables** = K1,700 ÷ K15,000 × 365 = **41 days**

**Days Payables** = K1,600 ÷ K9,200 × 365 = **63 days**

> **CCC = 103 + 41 − 63 = 81 days**
>
> Zambeef waits 81 days between paying for inputs and collecting cash from sales. Shorter = better.

## 2. The Cost of Ignoring a Cash Discount

A supplier offers **2/10 net 30** — pay within 10 days, get 2% off. Or pay the full amount in 30 days.

Sounds small. Run the numbers and it's a 37% loan.

### The Formula

**Discount Cost = [D ÷ (100 − D)] × [365 ÷ (N − T)]**

Where D = discount %, N = net days, T = discount period days

### Example: 2/10 net 30

**[2 ÷ 98] × [365 ÷ 20] = 37.2% annualised cost**

/info
If you can borrow at 8%, always take the discount. Paying late on 2/10 net 30 costs the equivalent of borrowing at 37%.

## 3. Segregation of Duties

The most tested treasury control in every past paper.

**The rule:** the person who authorises a transaction cannot be the same person who records it.

### Why it matters — Barings Bank, 1995

/warning
Nick Leeson traded **and** confirmed his own positions with no oversight. By the time anyone noticed, he had hidden £800 million in losses. Barings Bank — 233 years old — collapsed in weeks.

**One control missing. One bank gone.**

### In the exam

You'll get a scenario and be asked to identify what's wrong. If one person has too much access — that's your answer.
